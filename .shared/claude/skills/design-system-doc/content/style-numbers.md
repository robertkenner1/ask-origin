# Numbers

**Numbers** mean the symbols and words that represent measurements, counts, and values. This section is organized alphabetically and applies to content in product UI.

For a full breakdown of Superhuman's approach to grammar and usage in other external-facing content, visit the Superhuman Company Style Guide.

## But first, a note about localization

We write for the Superhuman product using American English spelling, grammar, and formatting for text and numbers. Although a user‚Äôs writing *is* corrected by Grammarly Proofreader agent based on the dialect of English they choose, the rest of the UI is not localized into either English dialects or other languages. Learn about localization and internationalization.

Despite this, it‚Äôs important to consider how machine or AI translations by third-party apps and browser extensions could affect our product. For example, certain browser extensions can translate text in UI from English into a different language.

To compensate for this and maximize comprehension:

* Use consistent terminology when applicable
* Write in American English with American formatting
* Apply standards from Origin and the [Company Brand Style Guide](https://coda.io/d/Grammarly-Style-Guide_dNw1zZnMEND/Grammarlys-Plans_su4UNXs7)

Keep global political perspectives front of mind when writing for the product. Grammarly‚Äôs audience is, in part, people who learn English as a second language. Look for what could be misunderstood and edit it for complete clarity.

## Always use numerals

Always use numerals in the product. Numerals are the symbols that represent numbers‚Äîthat means 0‚Äì9 and 10‚Äì10,000, too. Follow American numbering conventions. For example, use a decimal point over a decimal comma.

## Currency

Above all, be crystal clear about the monetary value you are describing, especially when relaying to a user how much something costs or how much they will spend. Consider, for example, that the *$* symbol can refer to US dollars, Australian dollars, Canadian dollars, Mexican pesos, and a few other currencies, too.

### Format

Because it‚Äôs crucial that customers know what currency we're referring to, use the currency symbol as well as the internationally recognized, three-letter ISO code. Notably, the ISO currency code for euros is  ‚ÄúEUR,‚Äù regardless of country.

**Do not** assume a currency symbol alone will communicate the country or currency in which a user is making their transaction.

**Do not** write out monetary values as a sentence, mix numerals and written-out values, or vaguely hint at approximate values.

**Do not** include a space between the currency symbol and the numeral except when the symbol comes after the value.

### Decimals

Always include decimals to the hundredth place. Even if a monetary value is whole (without cents), use ‚Äú.00‚Äù at the end for precision and consistency in formatting. Include decimals for zero values as well.

Do not shorten a decimal to the tenth place for any reason. Likewise, do not give values with fractions of cents.

#### Exceptions

* **For whole values in a heading (h1, h2, h3, etc.):** It‚Äôs OK to omit decimals to save space. One example of this is the plan comparison page, where prices are listed as h3 headings without decimals because they‚Äôre all whole values. If any of the three had cents, then all values should use decimals for consistency.
* **Some currencies do not have cents**, such as the Japanese Yen, while others include cents to the thousandth place, such as the Kuwaiti Dinar.  While Grammarly does not accept payments in these currencies today, be sure to follow the local standards by currency and location to avoid unfamiliar formats.

#### Comma for thousands, period for decimals

In most English-speaking countries, commas mark the thousands place of whole numbers. Periods delineate the whole numbers from fractions of currency (cents). The symbol comes before the value without a space in between.

Use this separator format when displaying currency in the United States, Mexico, United Kingdom, Ireland, Liechtenstein, Malta, and Canada when writing in English.

#### Period for thousands, comma for decimals

In most European countries and regions influenced by continental European conventions, periods will mark the thousands place of whole currency while commas delineate cents. The currency symbol usually follows the amount and is preceded by a space.

Use this separator format when displaying currency in Europe except where noted previously, as well as Canada if writing in French.

#### Indian numbering system

The Indian numbering system uses a comma separator after every two digits, except for the last section of whole currency, which is three digits. Decimals are given to the hundredth place.

### Negative currency

#### Minus sign

Use a minus sign to indicate a negative monetary value. Always place the minus sign at the start. **Do not** include a space between the minus sign and the currency symbol or value, depending on the format.

**Do not** place a currency value inside of parentheses without a minus sign. Although this format is standard in accounting and finance, it can be unfamiliar to people outside these fields.

Be vigilant in how you write or code content‚Äîa hyphen or dash is often misused where a minus sign should be. Screen readers more reliably announce the minus sign and will convey the correct meaning for negative currency.

#### Color

Color can be used in addition to a minus sign, but the use of color alone does not meet accessibility standards. Learn about color and accessibility.

## Percentages

Always use numerals for percentages‚Äîin product and in most external-facing writing. Use a percent sign. Do not add a space between the numeral and the percent sign.

### Significant figures

When deciding how to use decimals, ask yourself:

* Is your statistic absolutely exact or is it more like an educated guess?
* How much precision does the user need to make an informed choice?

To avoid the impression that a rough estimate is an unimpeachable fact, consider rounding up or down to the nearest whole number for inexact stats and percentages.

If your use case warrants precise metrics, write out statistics and percentages to a maximum of 2 decimal places. Display more precise data only when absolutely necessary to convince or inform.

## Suggestion counts

When displaying the number of suggestions available, use numerals. Follow our general numbering style and include commas every three digits to the left of the decimal point.

If there are zero suggestions, use ‚ÄúNo suggestions.‚Äù

## Times

Use numerals and include the hour and minute digits.

Abbreviate AM and PM in all caps, without periods. Make sure there is a space between the hour/minute digits and the AM and PM abbreviations.

For noon and midnight, spelling it out more clearly (12 noon; 12 midnight) is preferred in places like body text or when there is more space. But if we must follow the # AM/# PM format, style as such: 12 PM (noon) and 12 AM (midnight).
