## Context on disabilities

Disabilities are not rare. In the U.S. alone, about <Link href="https://archive.cdc.gov/#/details?url=https://www.cdc.gov/media/releases/2018/p0816-disability.html">25% of the adult population lives with a disability</Link>. 

The <Link href="https://www.cdc.gov/ncbddd/disabilityandhealth/disability.html">CDC</Link> defines a disability as ‚Äúany condition of the body or mind (impairment) that makes it more difficult for the person with the condition to do certain activities (activity limitation) and interact with the world around them (participation restrictions).‚Äù

Everyone experiences disabilities in a different way. Obviously, it would be impossible to capture the whole of this complex human experience in this guide, nor would it be appropriate to try. However, some context about disabilities is helpful to keep in mind when designing accessible products.

### Disability can be permanent, temporary, or situational

Everyone experiences some degree of disability at some point in their life. It may be a permanent disability, like ADHD. It may be a temporary disability, like a broken leg. Or it may be a situational disability, like poor cognitive functioning due to lack of sleep.

### Assistive technology vs. adaptive strategies

Our goal is to design products that work with assistive technology (like screen readers) as well as adaptive strategies‚Äîthe ways a person might change their environment to make it more accessible. 

**Assistive technology (AT):** Specialized software or hardware that helps people perceive and understand their environment. A screen reader, for example, will read the content of websites aloud or convert it to braille for a person who is blind.

**Adaptive strategies:** Preference settings in browsers, apps, and operating systems that can customize a user‚Äôs experience. Someone with low vision, for example, may magnify a web page by 400% to read the information. 

### Categories of disability

When designing for accessibility, it‚Äôs helpful to think about five categories.

## Organizing information

Presenting information in a logical, consistent manner helps everyone‚Äîdisabled or not‚Äîunderstand what they need to know. These techniques are particularly helpful for designing accessible content.

### Signposting

Signposts help a user navigate content by indicating where they are and what to expect next. Signposts may take the form of:

- Informative headings and logical heading hierarchy for content sections
- Breadcrumbs that indicate the user‚Äôs navigation path
- Screen reader‚Äìonly content to label or describe sections that are only distinguished visually

### Consistency

Consistency, the agreement of parts among a whole, is critical for helping users understand and process information. Content should follow the pattern a user expects based on precedent or general convention. For instance:

- Parallel structure in headings, lists, and other components
- Consistent typography, casing, styling, and length
- Standard names for buttons and settings

#### What not to do

Steam, the leading storefront and platform for PC gaming, has unpredictable content in menus and buttons across their features for web, mobile, and desktop apps. Typography, colors, casing, and content are often inconsistent, creating a disorienting experience for all users‚Äînot just those with disabilities.

## Writing

When written with attention to accessibility, product content will naturally be clear and understandable‚Äîaccessible content helps everyone! Here are a few things to keep in mind:

- Aim for a 7th-grade reading level or lower. Shorter sentences and simpler vocabulary help reduce reading level.
- Avoid introducing proper names that are written in title case needlessly, for example ‚ÄúSelect your preferred Billing Cycle.‚Äù They increase cognitive load for users, dilute brand equity, and are difficult to maintain consistently across surfaces.
- Use active voice wherever possible and appropriate.
- Choose words carefully. Avoid exclusionary, offensive terms like ‚Äúblacklist‚Äù and ‚Äúwhitelist.‚Äù Notably, Grammarly does not use ‚Äúdisabled‚Äù or ‚Äúenabled‚Äù to describe active and inactive states in user-facing content. Refer to the <Link href="https://grammarly.atlassian.net/wiki/spaces/DES/pages/3814457527/Grammarly+Company+Style+Guide#Word-and-Term-List">Company Style Guide</Link> and [the GDS term list](/content/terminology) for more examples.

### Alt text

- Alt text allows users with visual impairments to understand information that is conveyed visually to sighted users through images.
- Purely decorative images don‚Äôt need alt text.
- If a photo or illustration adds key information, shows an example, or significantly contributes to the mood and tone of the page, it should have alt text.

### Spatial or directional descriptions

- ‚ÄúOpen the menu in the *top-left* corner.‚Äù 
- ‚ÄúUpdate your settings *below*.‚Äù 
- ‚ÄúSubmit your question using the form on the *right*.‚Äù 

These descriptions all rely on relative directions like above/below or left/right. **Avoid them**, because they don‚Äôt work for users who can‚Äôt see the page visually. They also may become inaccurate if the components on the page move around like with smaller screens or in a mobile layout.

Ideally, the page should be organized logically enough that you don‚Äôt need to describe exactly where everything is. If the page design is good, ‚ÄúOpen the menu,‚Äù ‚ÄúUpdate your settings,‚Äù or ‚ÄúSubmit your question using the form‚Äù should suffice.

### Naming and labeling

Assistive technology uses element names to help users navigate an interface. When writing element names, you should:

- Describe function, not appearance‚Äîfor example, ‚Äú**Edit** icon‚Äù not ‚Äú**Pen** icon‚Äù
- Lead with action verbs
- Be concise
- Use unique labels for each element unless they perform an identical function
- Begin each label with a capital letter and omit end punctuation

**Avoid emoji whenever possible.** They are inconsistent and unclear‚Äîtwo qualities to avoid in any user experience.

#### Inconsistencies by platform cause miscommunication

Emoji render very differently in apperance on different platforms, which causes miscommunications due to changes in meaning and emotion. 

Consider the different versions of the ‚Äú<Link href="https://emojipedia.org/nerd-face">nerd face</Link>‚Äù emoji. Recently, some platforms have acknowledged that bucktoothed representations evoke anti-Asian stereotypes. A beaming smile replaces the racist trope in Google‚Äôs updated version, giving it a more joyful and excited appearance than the others. Regardless, using this particular emoji in the product would never be worth the risk of offending Grammarly customers who use an Apple device. <Link href="https://grouplens.org/blog/investigating-the-potential-for-miscommunication-using-emoji/">Investigating the Potential for Miscommunication Using Emoji, Hannah Miller, 2016</Link>.
